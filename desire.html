Desire

Graduate education does not only shape what students know. It also shapes what they come to want, pursue, fear, and imagine as possible. Desire is not outside the institution. It is cultivated within it.

You arrive wanting something.

To write. To think. To become. To make a life around ideas.

The institution needs that desire.

It depends on intensity, commitment, hope, endurance, and attachment.

But desire does not remain untouched.

It is redirected toward metrics, timelines, productivity, and legibility.
Graduate students are asked to desire intensely— to write obsessively, think passionately, and dedicate themselves to intellectual life— while also being warned against excess, instability, or deviation.
The system needs drive, but not too much freedom.

What draws you in

Curiosity. Thought. Recognition. A project. A future. The hope that intellectual life might still be a way of living.

What desire becomes

Output. Professionalization. Self-management. Optimization. The slow redirection of longing into institutional form.

You begin to want what the discipline rewards.

Even when it narrows what you once thought possible.

And still, something exceeds capture.

A question that will not quiet down. A project that keeps widening. A force that resists being reduced to output.

Desire is not a flaw in the system.

It is one of the conditions that makes intellectual life possible.
